Why panic relief shouldn't cost $70 a year

The apps built for the worst five minutes of your week mostly charge $60–70 a year, by subscription. We think that model is wrong for this problem — not as a matter of taste, but on the published evidence. Here is that evidence.

The moment and the meter

A panic attack has a shape. Your chest tightens, your heart is suddenly loud, and some part of your brain starts narrating worst cases. It peaks in minutes. What helps in that window is small and well-known: slow the exhale, ground the senses, wait for the wave to pass.

Open the App Store in that moment and you will meet a different shape: the free trial. The category's standard price is $60–70 per year — Calm and Headspace at $69.99, Rootd at up to $79.99, Dare at $59.99 to $69.99 (US prices, July 2026). The tool for a five-minute emergency is rented by the month, like a gym you're supposed to keep attending.

That pricing assumes something about you: that you'll keep coming back, week after week, all year. The industry's own data says you won't.

What the retention data actually shows

In 2019, researchers led by Amit Baumel measured real-world usage of 93 popular mental health apps — not what users say, but what they do. The median app kept 3.9% of its users by day 15, and 3.3% by day 30.[1]

For apps built around breathing exercises, measured 30-day retention was zero percent. Not low. Zero.[1]

Subscription businesses know this. Roughly 30% of annual app subscriptions are cancelled within the first month.[2] The economics still work — because an annual price collected in January doesn't care whether you open the app in March. For most consumer apps that's merely cynical. For a panic tool it inverts the product's purpose: the revenue model performs best when the user stops showing up, and the user stopping is priced in from the start.

None of this means the apps are bad. Several are genuinely good — we've said so, with their strengths, in our own comparison. It means the meter is running against a usage pattern that everyone involved can predict.

Panic doesn't come monthly

Here's the mismatch underneath it. Panic is episodic by nature. In the largest cross-national study of panic attacks — nearly 143,000 people across 25 countries — attack frequency ranged from several per day to a few per year, and among people with lifetime panic disorder, only 57% had episodes in the previous twelve months.[3] About 2.7% of U.S. adults experience panic disorder in a given year.[4]

An episodic problem meets a tool you need intensely, briefly, and then perhaps not for months. A subscription bills you evenly through the quiet stretches — you pay most, per use, in exactly the periods you're doing well. It is renting an umbrella by the month in a climate where it rains four times a year.

The cost is not hypothetical

It would be easy to wave this off — "it's the price of a coffee a month" — if cost weren't a documented barrier to exactly this kind of help. In 2025, 6% of U.S. adults reported going without needed mental health care because of cost, up from 4% in 2021.[5] Among uninsured people with serious mental health problems, 64% report cost-related barriers.[6] The people most likely to bounce off a $70 paywall are not the ones who needed it least.

What actually helps doesn't require a service

The techniques inside these apps — paced breathing, grounding, brief cognitive exercises — are public knowledge with real evidence behind them. Brief, self-guided formats hold up in trials: a four-session panic intervention showed large, sustained effects (Cohen's d = 2.65);[7] a meta-analysis of 25 randomized trials of self-guided digital interventions found reliable benefits;[8] even single-session formats have supporting RCTs.[9]

Meanwhile, a review of 73 mental health apps found 64% claiming effectiveness — while 1.4% cited any published research for those claims.[10] The category talks science and prices like streaming.

A breathing pacer is not a service with marginal costs per user per month. It is a tool. Tools have a price shape that everyone already understands: you pay for it once, like a book, and it's yours.

The shape we think is honest

So, concretely, what would honest pricing for a panic tool look like?

Free at the moment of need — because the worst moment to meet a paywall is the one this product exists for. Paid once, if you find it useful — because a tool should earn its money on merit, not on your forgetting to cancel. And no meter — because your quiet months shouldn't be someone's best revenue.

To be clear, this shape already exists in corners of the market: PanicShield is entirely free, Medito runs as a free nonprofit, and Apple's own Watch does haptic breathing at no cost. If your budget is zero, use them — they're good.

A disclosure, kept to the end on purpose: we make Reground — 22 techniques behind a Lock Screen widget, six of them free forever, the rest unlocked for $5.99, once. No account, no analytics, no subscription. We wrote this essay because we priced the app this way, and we priced the app this way because of the evidence above — not the other way around.
